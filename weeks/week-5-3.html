Week 5.3: Balancing Chemical Equations

← Back to Part V: Introduction to Physical and Chemical Science

Objective

  • Understand the Law of Conservation of Mass and why equations must be balanced.
  • Learn the difference between subscripts (part of the molecule) and coefficients (multipliers).
  • Apply algebraic concepts, like the distributive property, to balance chemical equations.

Tools

  • Notebook, pencils, and erasers (you will do a lot of erasing!).
  • Scratch paper or a mini whiteboard for tallying atoms.
  • A digital balancing equations simulator (optional, e.g., PhET Interactive Simulations).

Content

The Law of Conservation of Mass

In chemistry, there is a golden rule: Matter cannot be created or destroyed. This means that if you start a chemical reaction with 10 atoms of Hydrogen, you MUST end the reaction with exactly 10 atoms of Hydrogen. They might be arranged into new molecules, but the total number stays the same!

Subscripts vs. Coefficients (The Math Connection!)

To balance equations, you need to understand two types of numbers:

  • Subscripts: The tiny numbers attached to an element (like the "2" in H₂O). You cannot change these, or you change the actual chemical!
  • Coefficients: The big numbers placed in front of a molecule (like the "3" in 3H₂O). This is exactly like the distributive property in algebra! The 3 multiplies everything in the molecule. 3H₂O means you have 3 water molecules, which gives you 6 Hydrogen atoms (3 × 2) and 3 Oxygen atoms (3 × 1).

Steps to Balance an Equation

Let's look at making water: H₂ + O₂ → H₂O

  1. Count the atoms on both sides:
    Left (Reactants): H = 2, O = 2
    Right (Products): H = 2, O = 1 (Uh oh, we lost an Oxygen! This is unbalanced.)
  2. Add coefficients to balance: Let's put a "2" in front of H₂O to get 2 Oxygen atoms on the right.
    H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
    Now count again: Left: H=2, O=2. Right: H=4, O=2. (Now Hydrogen is unbalanced!)
  3. Adjust again: Put a "2" in front of the H₂ on the left.
    2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
    Final count: Left: H=4, O=2. Right: H=4, O=2. Balanced!

Challenges

  1. The Distributive Property Review:

    Calculate the total number of atoms for each element in the following molecules using coefficients:

    • a) 4 CO₂ (How many Carbon? How many Oxygen?)
    • b) 3 C₆H₁₂O₆ (How many of each?)
    • c) 2 Al₂(SO₄)₃ (Careful! Multiply the outside coefficient, and the subscript outside the parentheses!)
  2. Balancing Act Level 1:

    Copy these unbalanced equations onto a piece of scratch paper. Use a pencil to add coefficients until both sides have the exact same number of each type of atom.

    • a) Na + Cl₂ → NaCl
    • b) N₂ + H₂ → NH₃
  3. Balancing Act Level 2:

    Try balancing the combustion of methane (what happens when you light a gas stove):

    • CH₄ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O
    • Hint: Balance Carbon first, then Hydrogen, and save Oxygen for last!
