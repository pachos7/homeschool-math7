Week 1.5: Mean, Median, Mode, Range, and Average

← Back to Part I: Remembering Math Skills

Objective

  • Understand and calculate the mean (average) of a data set.
  • Find the median (middle value) of ordered data.
  • Identify the mode (most frequent value) in a data set.
  • Calculate the range to understand data spread.
  • Apply these statistical measures to real-world scenarios.

Tools

  • Calculator (physical or digital)
  • Mean, Median, Mode & Range Calculator
  • Paper and pencil for organizing data
  • Spreadsheet software (Google Sheets or Excel) for larger data sets

Content

📚 Key Topics

  • Mean (Average): Add all numbers together, then divide by how many numbers there are
  • Median: The middle number when data is arranged in order (for even numbers, average the two middle values)
  • Mode: The number that appears most frequently (there can be no mode, one mode, or multiple modes)
  • Range: The difference between the largest and smallest values

📖 Examples

Data Set: 3, 7, 5, 13, 20, 23, 39, 23, 40, 23, 14, 12, 56, 23, 29

  • Mean: Sum = 330, Count = 15, Mean = 330 ÷ 15 = 22
  • Median: Ordered: 3, 5, 7, 12, 13, 14, 20, 23, 23, 23, 23, 29, 39, 40, 56 → Median = 23
  • Mode: 23 appears 4 times (most frequent) → Mode = 23
  • Range: 56 - 3 = 53

💡 Real-World Connection

These statistical measures help you:

  • Understand test scores and grade averages in school
  • Analyze sports statistics (batting averages, points per game)
  • Make sense of weather data (average temperatures, rainfall)
  • Compare prices when shopping (average cost, price range)
  • Understand news reports with data and statistics

Challenges

  1. Basic Practice: Find the mean, median, mode, and range for this data set: 8, 12, 15, 12, 9, 10, 12, 18
  2. Real-World Data: Record the daily high temperature for one week. Calculate all four measures of central tendency.
  3. Sports Statistics: Look up the points scored by your favorite basketball player in their last 10 games. Find the mean, median, mode, and range.
  4. Challenge Problem: A student has test scores of 85, 92, 78, and 90. What score does the student need on the fifth test to have a mean of 87?
  5. Discussion: When would the median be more useful than the mean? Think about situations with outliers (extreme values).
  6. Create Your Own: Collect data from your classmates (favorite number, shoe size, etc.) and calculate all four statistical measures.
