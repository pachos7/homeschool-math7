Objective

  • Understand the basic purpose of statistics: collecting, analyzing, and interpreting data.
  • Differentiate between categorical (qualitative) and numerical (quantitative) data.
  • Distinguish between continuous and discrete numerical data.

Tools

  • Notebook and pencils
  • Real-world data examples (e.g., weather reports, sports stats, grocery receipts)
  • Measuring tools (ruler, measuring tape, scale, stopwatch)

Content

What is Statistics? Statistics is the science of collecting, organizing, analyzing, and interpreting data in order to make decisions or draw conclusions about the world around us.

Video Resource: What Is Statistics: Crash Course Statistics #1 (YouTube)

Types of Data

When we collect data, it generally falls into two main categories:

  • Categorical (Qualitative) Data: Describes qualities or characteristics. It does not have numerical meaning. (Examples: eye color, favorite type of pizza, car brands).
  • Numerical (Quantitative) Data: Deals with numbers and things you can measure or count. Numerical data is further broken down into two types: Discrete and Continuous.

Discrete vs. Continuous Data

  • Discrete Data: Information that can only take on specific, separate values. You usually count discrete data. It cannot be broken down into smaller parts or fractions.
    Examples: The number of students in a class, the number of goals in a soccer game, the number of cars passing by. You can't have 2.5 students or 3.7 cars!
  • Continuous Data: Information that can be measured and can take on any value within a range. You usually measure continuous data. It can include decimals and fractions.
    Examples: The height of a tree, the exact weight of a dog, the amount of time it takes to run a mile. A person could be 5 feet, 5.2 feet, or 5.234 feet tall!

Challenges

  1. Identify the Data Type:

    Look at the list of data collection scenarios below. For each one, determine if the data collected would be Categorical, Discrete Numerical, or Continuous Numerical.

    • a) The color of the cars in a parking lot.
    • b) The weight of the pumpkins grown in a garden.
    • c) The number of pages in the different books on your shelf.
    • d) The temperature of the swimming pool each morning.
    • e) The number of points scored during a basketball game.
  2. Your Own Data Collection:

    Explore your house or neighborhood and find real-life examples of data. Write down:

    • Two examples of Discrete Data you could collect.
    • Two examples of Continuous Data you could measure.

    Explain why each example fits into that category. (Hint: Think about whether you are counting or measuring!)
