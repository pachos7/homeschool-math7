Week 4.5: Concept of Change and Rates

← Back to Part IV: Mathematical Change and Function Concepts

Objective

  • Understand the concept of rate of change as a ratio that describes how one quantity changes in relation to another.
  • Calculate the constant rate of change from tables, graphs, and real-world word problems.
  • Differentiate between positive, negative, zero, and undefined rates of change.

Tools

  • Graph paper, rulers, and pencils
  • Basic calculators
  • Interactive online graphing tools like Desmos or GeoGebra

Content

Introduction to Rate of Change: Rate of change is a fundamental mathematical concept that shows how one variable alters as another variable increases or decreases. It is commonly written as a ratio of the change in the dependent variable (usually y) to the change in the independent variable (usually x).

Video Resource: Introduction to Rate of Change (YouTube)

Key Concepts

  • Constant Rate of Change: When the rate of change between any two points in a linear relationship remains the same, it is referred to as constant.
  • Formula: Rate of Change = change in y / change in x (or rise over run).
  • Visualizing Change: A steep line on a graph indicates a fast rate of change, while a shallow line indicates a slower rate. A horizontal line has a rate of change of zero.

Practice Focus

Students should practice interpreting rates of change from distance-time graphs (speed as the rate of change) and cost-quantity data tables.

  2. Graph Plotting & Interpretation:

    Plot the following points on a coordinate plane to create a distance-time graph illustrating a car's journey: (0, 0), (2, 130), (3, 195), and (5, 325). Let the x-axis represent Time (hours) and the y-axis represent Distance (miles).

    • What is the car's speed (rate of change) during the first 2 hours?
    • Calculate the rate of change between hour 3 and hour 5. Does the car maintain a constant speed throughout the trip?
  3. Word Problem:

    A large 500-gallon swimming pool begins to drain at a constant rate. If the pool loses 150 gallons of water in 3 hours:

    • What is its rate of change in gallons per hour? (Remember, since it's losing water, the rate of change will be negative!)
    • Write an equation to represent the volume of water y remaining in the pool after x hours.
