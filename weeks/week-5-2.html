Content

What is an Atom?

Everything in the universe is made of tiny building blocks called atoms. Atoms are incredibly small, but they are made up of even smaller pieces called subatomic particles:

  • Protons: These have a positive charge (+) and are located in the center of the atom, called the nucleus.
  • Neutrons: These have no charge (neutral) and are also located in the nucleus.
  • Electrons: These have a negative charge (-) and zip around the nucleus in shells or orbitals.

Video Resource: The Nucleus: Crash Course Chemistry #1 (YouTube)

The Periodic Table & Math

The Periodic Table organizes all the elements. Each box gives us mathematical clues about the atom's structure:

  • Atomic Number: This is the number at the top of the box. It tells you exactly how many protons the atom has. (In a neutral atom, it also tells you the number of electrons!)
  • Atomic Mass: The number at the bottom (usually a decimal). If you round it to the nearest whole number, it tells you the total number of particles in the nucleus (Protons + Neutrons).

Math equation to find Neutrons: Rounded Atomic Mass - Atomic Number = Number of Neutrons

Chemical Formulas as Ratios

When atoms bond together, they form molecules. We write these as chemical formulas, which are basically mathematical ratios.

For example, water is H₂O. This means water is made of Hydrogen (H) and Oxygen (O) in a specific ratio:

  • The little "2" means there are 2 Hydrogen atoms.
  • Because there is no number after the O, we assume it's a 1. So, there is 1 Oxygen atom.
  • The ratio of Hydrogen to Oxygen in water is 2:1.

If you change the ratio, you change the molecule! H₂O₂ (a 2:2 ratio) is hydrogen peroxide, which is completely different from water.

Challenges

  1. Atom Math:

    Look up the following elements on a periodic table. Find their Atomic Number and round their Atomic Mass to the nearest whole number. Then, calculate the number of protons, neutrons, and electrons for a neutral atom of each:

    • a) Carbon (C)
    • b) Oxygen (O)
    • c) Gold (Au)
  2. Molecule Ratios:

    Look at the chemical formulas below. Write down the elements involved and the ratio of their atoms.

    • a) Table Salt: NaCl
    • b) Carbon Dioxide: CO₂
    • c) Glucose (Sugar): C₆H₁₂O₆
  3. Scaling Up Ratios:

    Methane gas has the formula CH₄. The ratio of Carbon to Hydrogen is 1:4.

    If you have 5 molecules of Methane, how many Carbon atoms do you have? How many Hydrogen atoms? Write the new ratio.

  4. Atomic Algebra (Solving for Unknowns):

    Let's use our algebra skills from Week 3! We know the formula: Atomic Mass = Protons + Neutrons. Let M be Atomic Mass, p be Protons, and n be Neutrons. Write an algebraic equation for each scenario and solve for the unknown variable:

    • a) An atom has an atomic mass of 39 and 20 neutrons. How many protons does it have? (Find p)
    • b) An atom has 15 protons and an atomic mass of 31. How many neutrons does it have? (Find n)
  5. Chemical Percentages:

    Leveraging our percentage skills from Week 2! A single water molecule (H₂O) has an approximate total mass of 18 atomic mass units (amu). The oxygen atom contributes 16 amu to this total, and the two hydrogen atoms contribute 2 amu together.

    • a) What percentage of the water molecule's total mass is made up of Oxygen?
    • b) What percentage is made up of Hydrogen?
  6. Graphing the Elements (Coordinate Plane):

    Let's use our graphing skills from Week 4! Look up the first 5 elements on the periodic table (Hydrogen, Helium, Lithium, Beryllium, Boron).

    • a) Create a data table with the "Atomic Number" as the x-coordinate and the "Number of Neutrons" as the y-coordinate.
    • b) Plot these 5 points on a coordinate plane. What pattern do you notice as the atomic number increases?
