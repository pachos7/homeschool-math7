Objective

  • Understand the three laws of motion by Sir Isaac Newton.
  • Apply mathematical concepts to physical forces, mass, and acceleration.
  • Perform practical experiments to observe the laws of physics in real life.

Tools

  • A drinking cup, a stiff piece of cardboard (like a playing card), and a coin.
  • Two objects of different weights (e.g., a tennis ball and a heavy book).
  • A balloon, string, a drinking straw, and some tape.
  • Measuring tape and a stopwatch (a smartphone timer works!).
  • Notebook and calculator.

Content

Welcome to Part V! We are going to explore how math applies to the physical world around us by diving into Sir Isaac Newton's Three Laws of Motion.

Intro to Newton's Laws

Newton's laws explain how objects move and why they move. They are the foundation of classical mechanics and are deeply connected to mathematical equations.

Video Resource: Intro to Newton's Laws of Motion (YouTube)

Newton's First Law (Inertia)

The Law: An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion with the same speed and in the same direction unless acted upon by an unbalanced force.

Video Resource: Newton's First Law of Motion (YouTube)

Newton's Second Law (F = ma)

The Law: The acceleration of an object depends on the mass of the object and the amount of force applied. This is famously written as the mathematical equation: Force (F) = mass (m) × acceleration (a).

Video Resource: Newton's Second Law of Motion (YouTube)

Newton's Third Law (Action and Reaction)

The Law: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

Video Resource: Newton's Third Law of Motion (YouTube)

Challenges

  1. Challenge 1: The First Law Coin Drop (Inertia)

    Setup: Place a stiff card (like a playing card) flat over the top of a drinking cup. Place a coin in the center of the card.

    Action: Flick the card horizontally as fast as you can.

    Math / Physics: The card flies away, but the coin drops straight into the cup! Because of inertia, the coin wants to stay at rest. The force was only applied to the card.

  2. Challenge 2: The Second Law Push (F = ma)

    Setup: Find a light object (like a tennis ball) and a heavier object (like a hardcover book or a basketball).

    Action: Try to push both objects across the floor using the exact same amount of force. Watch what happens to their acceleration. The lighter object should accelerate much faster!

    Math / Physics: Let's use the formula F = m × a to see why.

    • Math Question 1: If you push a toy car with a mass (m) of 2 kg, and you use a force (F) of 10 Newtons, what is its acceleration (a)?
    • Math Question 2: Now try pushing a box with a mass (m) of 5 kg with the exact same 10 Newtons of force. What is its new acceleration? Which one has higher acceleration?
  3. Challenge 3: The Third Law Balloon Rocket

    Setup: Thread a long string through a drinking straw. Tape the ends of the string tightly across a room. Tape a blown-up (but untied) balloon to the straw.

    Action: Let the balloon go! The air pushes backwards out of the balloon (Action), which pushes the balloon forward along the string (Reaction).

    Math / Physics: Let's calculate the speed of the balloon's reaction.

    • Math Question: Measure the total distance (d) in feet (or meters) that your balloon traveled. Use a stopwatch to time exactly how many seconds (t) it took to travel that far. What was the average speed of your balloon rocket? (Speed = Distance ÷ Time).
