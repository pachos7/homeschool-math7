Week 3.4: Angles and Coordinate Geometry

← Back to Part III: Algebra and Geometry Foundations

Objective

  • Identify and classify angles (acute, right, obtuse, straight, reflex).
  • Understand complementary and supplementary angles.
  • Plot and read points on the coordinate plane in all four quadrants.
  • Connect the idea of angles with geometric shapes on the coordinate plane.
  • Apply coordinate geometry to solve real-world problems.

Tools

  • Protractor and ruler
  • Graph paper or printable coordinate graph paper (PDF)
  • Notebook for notes and sketches
  • Pencil and colored pencils (optional, for labeling quadrants)

Content

📐 Types of Angles

  • Acute angle: Less than 90°
  • Right angle: Exactly 90°
  • Obtuse angle: Between 90° and 180°
  • Straight angle: Exactly 180°
  • Reflex angle: Greater than 180°

🔗 Angle Relationships

  • Complementary angles: Two angles that add up to 90°
  • Supplementary angles: Two angles that add up to 180°
  • Vertical angles: Opposite angles formed by two intersecting lines — they are always equal

🗺️ The Coordinate Plane

The coordinate plane is made of two number lines that cross at right angles:

  • x-axis: Horizontal line
  • y-axis: Vertical line
  • Origin: The point (0, 0) where the axes meet
  • Quadrants: The four regions created by the axes (I, II, III, IV)

Every point is written as an ordered pair (x, y). The x-value tells you how far to move left or right; the y-value tells you how far to move up or down.

💡 Real-World Connection

  • Architects use angles every day when designing buildings
  • GPS systems use coordinate geometry to locate positions
  • Pilots and sailors use angles to navigate their routes
  • Video game designers use coordinate planes to place objects on screen

Challenges

  1. Use a protractor to draw and label one example of each angle type: acute, right, obtuse, straight, and reflex.
  2. Find the missing angle: If two angles are complementary and one measures 37°, what is the other? If two angles are supplementary and one measures 112°, what is the other?
  3. Plot the following points on a coordinate plane and label each one: A(3, 4), B(-2, 5), C(-4, -1), D(6, -3), E(0, 2).
  4. Identify which quadrant each point falls in: (5, 7), (-3, 2), (-6, -4), (1, -8).
  5. Creative Challenge: On graph paper, draw a simple house or rocket ship using at least 6 points. List the coordinates of each corner and identify any right angles in your drawing.
  6. Real-World: Look at a city map or neighborhood photo. Can you spot coordinate grid-like patterns in streets? Describe what you find.
